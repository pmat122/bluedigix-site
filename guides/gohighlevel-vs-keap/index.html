A Brief History: Why Keap Has Two Names

Before we compare features, it is worth understanding why you will see "Keap" and "Infusionsoft" used interchangeably across the internet. They are the same company.

Infusionsoft launched in 2001 and became one of the first CRM and marketing automation platforms built specifically for small businesses. By the early 2010s, Infusionsoft had become the dominant platform for coaches, consultants, and info-product businesses that needed sophisticated email automation and e-commerce. The platform earned a reputation for being incredibly powerful -- and incredibly complicated. The campaign builder was a visual flowchart system that could do almost anything, but it took weeks or months to learn properly. Many businesses hired dedicated "Infusionsoft Certified Partners" just to set up their account.

In 2019, the company rebranded from Infusionsoft to Keap. The goal was to simplify the product, lower the entry barrier, and shed the reputation of being overly complex. The core automation engine carried over, but the interface was modernized and the pricing tiers were restructured. Long-time users still call it Infusionsoft. Newer users know it as Keap. Same company, same product lineage, different coat of paint.

Understanding this history matters because it explains both Keap's greatest strength (a mature, battle-tested automation engine built over two decades) and its biggest weakness (legacy complexity that still surfaces in the interface, the campaign builder, and the pricing model).

What Keap Does Well

Keap has been in the CRM and automation game longer than almost any competitor. That tenure shows in the depth of certain features that newer platforms, including GoHighLevel, have not yet matched.

Keap's Core Strengths

  • Established CRM with deep contact records: Keap's contact management is thorough. Every interaction -- emails opened, links clicked, forms submitted, purchases made, appointments attended, tags applied -- is logged in a detailed contact timeline. For businesses that need to see the full history of a client relationship at a glance, Keap's contact records are among the most detailed available for small business CRMs.
  • E-commerce and product sales: Keap has a built-in shopping cart, product catalog, order forms, and checkout pages. You can sell physical products, digital downloads, courses, and service packages directly through the platform. This is a significant advantage for service businesses that also sell products -- a nutritionist selling supplements, a consultant selling templates, a trainer selling courses alongside one-on-one coaching.
  • Invoicing and recurring billing: Keap handles invoicing natively. You can create line-item invoices, set up recurring billing for retainer clients, process credit card and ACH payments, and manage overdue invoice reminders -- all without a third-party tool. For service businesses where invoicing is a daily activity, this eliminates the need for a separate FreshBooks or QuickBooks subscription for basic billing.
  • Payment processing: Keap Pay (their native payment processor) and integrations with Stripe and PayPal mean you can collect payments through forms, invoices, checkout pages, and automated billing cycles. The payment data feeds directly into the CRM, so you can segment contacts by purchase history, total lifetime value, or payment status.
  • Automation campaign builder: The legacy Infusionsoft campaign builder -- while complex -- is genuinely powerful. You can build multi-branch automation sequences with conditional logic, delays, decision diamonds, goal triggers, and split testing. For businesses that have outgrown basic email sequences and need sophisticated if/then automation, Keap's campaign builder can handle nearly anything.
  • Appointment scheduling: Keap includes a built-in scheduling tool where prospects can book calls and meetings directly. The calendar integrates with the CRM so appointments show up in the contact record, and automated reminders reduce no-shows.
  • Accounting integrations: Native two-way syncs with QuickBooks and Xero keep your financial records clean without manual data entry. Invoices, payments, and refunds flow between Keap and your accounting software automatically.

CRM and Contact Management: How They Compare

Both platforms have functional CRMs, but they emphasize different things.

Keap's CRM is deeper on the contact record level. Each contact has a comprehensive timeline showing every email opened, link clicked, form submitted, product purchased, invoice paid, appointment attended, and automation triggered. The contact record also includes lead scoring (on the Max plan), which assigns numerical scores based on engagement behavior so you can prioritize your highest-intent prospects. For businesses that manage a smaller number of high-value client relationships, this level of detail is valuable.

GoHighLevel's CRM is more action-oriented. The contact record shows communication history (calls, texts, emails), pipeline stage, tags, and custom fields. The emphasis is less on passive tracking and more on what needs to happen next. Pipeline views, task assignments, and automated workflow triggers make GHL's CRM feel like a sales tool rather than a database. For businesses that are actively selling and need to move leads through a defined sales process, GHL's approach is more practical.

The honest assessment: if you have 200 clients and need to see every detail of each relationship, Keap's CRM gives you more data per contact. If you have 2,000 leads and need to see which ones are hot, which ones need follow-up, and which pipeline stage everyone sits in, GHL's CRM is more useful day-to-day.

Automation: Campaign Builder vs Workflow Builder

Both Keap and GoHighLevel are automation-first platforms. But their automation builders take fundamentally different approaches.

Keap's campaign builder is inherited from Infusionsoft. It is a visual canvas where you draw flowcharts connecting sequences, decision points, goals, and triggers. You can build incredibly complex multi-branch automations with conditional splits ("Did they open the email? Yes: send follow-up A. No: wait 3 days and send follow-up B"). The campaign builder can handle nested logic, goal-based completion triggers, and split testing within sequences. It is powerful -- but the learning curve is steep. First-time users regularly describe it as overwhelming, and many Keap customers hire a certified partner just to set up their initial campaigns.

GoHighLevel's workflow builder is newer and more intuitive. It uses a linear visual builder where you add triggers, actions, conditions, and wait steps in sequence. You can branch workflows with if/then conditions, use "Go To" actions for looping, and trigger workflows from dozens of events (form submissions, pipeline changes, tag additions, booking confirmations, missed calls, and more). While GHL's builder may not match the raw complexity ceiling of Keap's campaign builder, it covers 95% of what service businesses actually need -- and you can build a complete lead nurture workflow in 30 minutes instead of 3 hours.

For most service businesses, GHL's workflow builder is not just "good enough" -- it is preferable because you will actually build and maintain your automations instead of letting them stagnate because the campaign builder is too complicated to edit.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Keap the same as Infusionsoft?

Yes. Infusionsoft rebranded to Keap in 2019. The company is the same, the core product is the same, and most of the automation engine carries over. Keap simplified the interface and pricing tiers during the rebrand, but long-time users will recognize the same underlying campaign builder and CRM architecture. If you see references to Infusionsoft in online discussions, blog posts, or forums, they are talking about the same platform now called Keap.

Can GoHighLevel handle invoicing and payments like Keap?

GoHighLevel has basic payment collection through Stripe integration -- you can accept payments on order forms, membership pages, and booking confirmations. However, it does not have a dedicated invoicing system with line items, recurring billing management, or accounting software sync the way Keap does. If invoicing is central to your business operations, you will need to pair GoHighLevel with a tool like QuickBooks, FreshBooks, or Stripe Billing for that function.

Which platform is better for a service business under $500K in revenue?

For most service businesses under $500K, GoHighLevel delivers more ROI per dollar spent. At $97/month with unlimited contacts, you get CRM, email and SMS automation, landing pages, review management, and booking calendars -- all the tools needed to generate and convert leads at the growth stage. Keap at $249/month limits you to 1,500 contacts and does not include landing pages, SMS, or review automation. The exception is if your business relies heavily on e-commerce, product sales, or complex invoicing workflows, where Keap's built-in commerce tools give it a meaningful edge.

Does GoHighLevel have e-commerce features like Keap?

GoHighLevel has basic e-commerce capabilities -- you can sell digital products, courses, and memberships through its funnel builder and accept payments via Stripe. However, it does not have a full e-commerce engine with product catalogs, inventory management, order fulfillment, or the kind of shopping cart functionality that Keap offers natively. If you sell physical products or run an online store alongside your service business, Keap or a dedicated e-commerce platform like Shopify is the better fit for that specific function.

Can I migrate from Keap to GoHighLevel without losing my data?

Yes, but it requires manual work or a migration service. You can export your contacts, tags, and basic deal data from Keap as CSV files and import them into GoHighLevel. However, automation workflows, email templates, and campaign sequences do not transfer automatically -- you will need to rebuild those in GHL's workflow builder. The good news is that GHL's workflow builder is more intuitive than Keap's legacy campaign builder, so most users find the rebuild takes less time than expected.
